Assignment 5 - Task 1: Robotic Sensing - LiDAR Utility, Limitations, and Error Handling
==========================================================================================

=== 1. LiDAR Utility and Constraints ===

--- Application ---
The primary use of a 1D LiDAR on a differential drive robot is obstacle detection and
distance measurement. It emits a laser beam in a single direction and measures the
distance to the nearest object, enabling the robot to detect walls, obstacles, or
boundaries directly in front of it and react accordingly (e.g., stop or turn).

For tracking the robot pose (position + orientation), a 1D LiDAR alone is insufficient.
Appropriate sensors for pose tracking include:
- Wheel encoders (odometry): measure wheel rotations to estimate displacement
- IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit): measures acceleration and angular velocity
- GPS: provides absolute position outdoors
- Camera (Visual Odometry): estimates pose from image sequences
- 2D/3D LiDAR combined with SLAM algorithms (e.g., gmapping, Cartographer)

--- Limitations ---
Three physical or environmental scenarios where a 1D LiDAR will fail:

1. Transparent or reflective surfaces: Glass walls or mirrors either absorb, scatter,
   or reflect the laser beam in unexpected directions, causing false or missing readings.

2. Low or uneven obstacles: A 1D LiDAR measures only at a fixed height. It cannot
   detect objects below or above its scan plane, such as a step, a hole in the floor,
   or an overhead obstacle.

3. Highly absorptive surfaces: Dark or matte black surfaces absorb the laser beam and
   return very weak or no signal, leading to incorrect "no obstacle" readings even when
   an object is present.

--- Mitigation ---
To overcome these blind spots, an RGB-D camera (depth camera, e.g., Intel RealSense
or Microsoft Kinect) would be the best complementary sensor modality:
- It provides a full 2D depth image, detecting obstacles at all heights in the field of view.
- It works on transparent surfaces using structured light or time-of-flight methods.
- It is unaffected by surface absorption since it uses infrared or structured light patterns.
Combined with the 1D LiDAR, the robot gains robust spatial awareness both in the
laser plane and above/below it.


=== 2. Error Analysis and Denoising ===

--- Systematic vs. Random Errors ---

Systematic Errors:
- Are consistent, repeatable, and predictable.
- Caused by a permanent bias in the sensor, such as a miscalibrated zero point,
  manufacturing tolerances, or a fixed offset.
- Example: A LiDAR always reads 5 cm less than the true distance due to a hardware offset.

Random Errors:
- Are unpredictable and vary from measurement to measurement.
- Caused by noise sources such as electrical interference, atmospheric fluctuations,
  or surface texture variations.
- Example: Repeated measurements of the same static wall return slightly different
  distance values each time.

--- Error Removal Techniques ---

Removing Random Errors:
- Filtering / Averaging: Take multiple measurements and compute the mean or median.
  A Gaussian filter or a moving average smooths out random fluctuations.
- Kalman Filter: Combines sensor measurements with a motion model to optimally
  estimate the true state while suppressing noise.

Removing Systematic Errors:
- Calibration: The sensor is compared against a known ground truth reference.
  A correction factor or offset is determined and applied to all future measurements.
  Example: Measuring a wall at a precisely known distance of 1.000 m and correcting
  the sensor output to match that value.

--- Calibration ---
Calibration is formally the process of removing Systematic Errors.
It involves measuring a known reference and adjusting the sensor model
(e.g., applying a bias correction or scale factor) so that the sensor output
matches the true physical value. This is a one-time or periodic procedure,
distinct from real-time noise filtering which addresses random errors.
