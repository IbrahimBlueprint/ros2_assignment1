============================================================
Task 1 - Robot Kinematics vs Dynamics, Forward vs Inverse
============================================================

Context
-------
The robot considered here is the simple two-wheel turtle robot
from Assignment 2. It is a differential-drive mobile robot:
two independently driven wheels (left and right) mounted on a
common axle. Its pose in the plane is described by (x, y, theta),
where (x, y) is the position of the robot in the world frame and
theta is its orientation (heading angle). Its control inputs are
the angular velocities of the two wheels: omega_L (left wheel)
and omega_R (right wheel). Let r be the wheel radius and L be
the distance between the two wheels.


1) Forward Kinematics
---------------------
Forward kinematics answers the question:
"Given the motion of the wheels (joint-level inputs), what is
 the resulting motion / pose of the robot in the world?"

It is a purely geometric relationship - no forces, no masses,
no torques are involved. We only relate the velocities of the
wheels to the velocity of the robot body.

For the differential-drive turtle:
    v     = r * (omega_R + omega_L) / 2      (linear velocity)
    omega = r * (omega_R - omega_L) / L      (angular velocity)

Then the pose is obtained by integrating over time:
    x_dot     = v * cos(theta)
    y_dot     = v * sin(theta)
    theta_dot = omega

So as a programmer, if I know how fast each wheel turns, forward
kinematics tells me where my turtle will end up and how it will
be oriented.


2) Inverse Kinematics
---------------------
Inverse kinematics answers the opposite question:
"Given a desired motion of the robot (a desired v and omega, or
 a desired target pose), what wheel velocities do I have to
 command?"

It is the inversion of the forward kinematics. For the turtle:
    omega_R = (2*v + omega*L) / (2*r)
    omega_L = (2*v - omega*L) / (2*r)

This is what we actually use in navigation: the planner gives us
a desired linear and angular velocity (e.g. via a /cmd_vel
Twist message), and inverse kinematics tells the controller how
fast to spin the left and right wheels.

Note: for a differential-drive robot, inverse kinematics has a
unique solution, because the system has exactly 2 inputs and
2 controllable velocity components (v, omega). For robotic arms
with many joints, inverse kinematics can have multiple solutions
or no solution at all.


3) Forward Dynamics
-------------------
Forward dynamics introduces forces, torques, masses and inertia
into the picture. It answers:
"Given the torques applied to the wheels (and external forces
 like friction), how does the robot actually accelerate and
 move over time?"

So instead of just mapping velocities to velocities, we map
forces/torques to accelerations. We need a physical model of
the robot:
    - mass m of the robot
    - moment of inertia I around its vertical axis
    - wheel inertia
    - friction, slipping, motor characteristics

Conceptually, given torques tau_L and tau_R on the wheels:
    M(q) * q_ddot + C(q, q_dot) * q_dot + F = tau

Forward dynamics integrates this equation to produce the actual
trajectory the robot follows under those torques. It is what a
physics simulator (Gazebo, Webots, ...) computes internally.


4) Inverse Dynamics
-------------------
Inverse dynamics is the opposite:
"Given a desired motion (desired accelerations / a desired
 trajectory of v and omega), what torques must the motors apply
 to the wheels to actually realize it, taking mass, inertia and
 friction into account?"

It is what a torque-level controller computes:
    tau = M(q) * q_ddot_desired + C(q, q_dot) * q_dot + F

For the turtle, inverse dynamics would tell us how much torque
the left and right motors must produce so that the robot
actually accelerates to the velocities commanded by inverse
kinematics, despite friction, mass and inertia.


Summary table
-------------
                    | Input                | Output
--------------------+----------------------+-------------------
Forward kinematics  | wheel velocities     | robot pose/velocity
Inverse kinematics  | desired robot motion | wheel velocities
Forward dynamics    | torques / forces     | resulting motion
Inverse dynamics    | desired motion       | required torques

Key distinction:
- KINEMATICS = geometry of motion only (no masses, no forces).
- DYNAMICS   = motion together with the forces/torques that
               cause it (Newton/Euler, Lagrange, ...).
- FORWARD    = from inputs/causes to resulting motion.
- INVERSE    = from desired motion back to the required inputs.

As the programmer of the two-wheel turtle robot:
- I use INVERSE KINEMATICS to convert a desired (v, omega)
  from the navigation stack into wheel commands omega_L,
  omega_R.
- I use FORWARD KINEMATICS (odometry) to estimate the current
  pose of the robot from the measured wheel rotations.
- I would use INVERSE DYNAMICS if I controlled the motors at
  the torque level and wanted to compensate for inertia and
  friction precisely.
- I would use FORWARD DYNAMICS inside a physics simulator to
  predict the real behavior of the turtle under given torques.
